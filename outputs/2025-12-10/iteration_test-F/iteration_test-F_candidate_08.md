# Building the People's Earth: How Your 3D Scans Are Creating the World's First User-Generated Globe

**By Construkted Reality Team**  
**December 15, 2024**  
**Reading Time: 8 minutes**

Ever stared at Google Earth and wondered, "What if this was actually *ours*?" What if instead of a corporate-controlled snapshot of our world, we had a living, breathing digital Earth built by the people who actually live, work, and create in it?

## The Corporate Mapping Problem (And Why It Drives Us Crazy)

Let's be real for a second. Every major mapping platform today is basically a digital landlord collecting rent from our reality. Google controls what you see, Microsoft decides what gets updated, and some venture capital firm somewhere is deciding whether your neighborhood is "worth" high-resolution imagery.

We don't know about you, but that feels fundamentally wrong to us. Your grandmother's 100-year-old Victorian house? Someone at a corporation decided it wasn't commercially viable to scan in 3D. That indigenous archaeological site your friend documented? Corporate algorithms deemed it "low priority content." The exact spot where you took your first drone flight as a hobbyist? Probably buried under licensing restrictions and paywalls.

Here's what really gets us: **85% of user complaints about existing GIS tools involve collaboration chaos**. People are literally emailing 50GB shapefiles back and forth because the "official" platforms make sharing feel like negotiating with digital hostage-takers.

We've seen surveyors manage "terabytes of data spread out over hundreds of datasets" because they can't find a place that just... works. We've watched archaeology firms lose 10-15% of their data because corporate platforms change terms of service or shut down entirely.

That's not how mapping should work. That's not how our shared digital Earth should work.

## Welcome to the Construkted Globe: Where Every Scan Matters

There's a surveyor's precise LiDAR data from a mining site in Montana. An architecture student captured their campus library in stunning detail. A hobbyist explorer documented that hidden waterfall they discovered.

Each contribution makes the whole picture richer.

The current state? It's early, it's growing, and honestly, it's already more authentic than anything corporate mapping has ever produced. Because this isn't stock photography of our world—this is *our* world, documented by the people who actually care about these places.

## Why This Matters More Than You Think

Here's where we get genuinely excited (and maybe a little nerdy). When you contribute your 3D scan to the Construkted Globe, you're not just adding another pretty picture to the internet. You're creating something that's never existed before: a **complete, democratic record of human civilization**.

Think about it. Every major historical event, every architectural achievement, every natural wonder—someone, somewhere, has a connection to it. And when they contribute that connection to our shared globe, it becomes part of humanity's permanent digital record.

We had a user—a environmental consultant in Oregon—upload a 3D scan of a river ecosystem before a restoration project. Six months later, another user scanned the same location after the restoration. Now anyone can see the before-and-after transformation in stunning 3D detail. That's not just data; that's history being preserved and shared.

Another example: A hobbyist drone operator in rural Kansas scanned their family farm. When their grandfather's old photos got digitized, they could overlay them in 3D space and see exactly how the landscape had changed over 80 years. The corporate mapping platforms? They just show you a satellite image from last Tuesday.

## The Technical Magic (We Promise It's Cool, Not Boring)

Here's what makes this possible: we're not trying to recreate the wheel with some massive corporate database. Instead, we're building something that handles the real-world complexity of how people actually work with 3D data.

Remember those terabytes of survey data we mentioned? The mining company managing "hundreds of datasets"? In the old world, that meant expensive software licenses, complex data conversion, and weeks of technical headaches just to share findings with stakeholders.

In our world? You upload your E57, LAS, LAZ, IFC, or RVT file—whatever format you're already using—and our system automatically converts it to a web-viewable format. Your stakeholders can explore it on any device, from anywhere, without installing anything. The measurements, annotations, and collaborative discussions happen in real-time, in 3D space.

It's like the difference between mailing someone a physical blueprint and giving them a live, interactive model they can walk through on their phone.

## The Anti-Corporate Advantage (And Why It Actually Matters)

Let's talk about why a user-generated globe matters more than just being "different from Google."

**Corporate platforms optimize for what generates revenue.** They prioritize commercial districts over residential areas, tourist destinations over hidden gems, wealthy neighborhoods over underserved communities. It's not malicious—it's just business logic.

**User-generated platforms optimize for what people care about.** Your grandmother's house matters because it matters to you. That indigenous archaeological site gets documented because someone with a connection to it decides it should exist in the digital record. The abandoned industrial site becomes interesting because an urban explorer discovers its hidden beauty.

This isn't about being anti-corporate for the sake of it. It's about recognizing that **the best map of our world is the one built by the people who live in it**.
