## The Corporate Mapping Problem We've All Been Stuck With

Let's be honest for a moment. When you look at Google Earth or Bing Maps, you're seeing the world through a corporate lens. These platforms decide what gets highlighted, what gets "updated," and what gets buried in the algorithmic depths. They choose which cities get high-resolution 3D models and which rural areas remain fuzzy blobs. They control the narrative of what our planet looks like, one carefully curated pixel at a time.

We've watched too many incredible places—abandoned industrial sites, urban exploration spots, small towns with character—remain invisible in these corporate maps. Why? Because they don't fit the monetization strategy. Because they don't serve the advertising ecosystem. Because some corporation decided they weren't "valuable enough" to document properly.

But here's the thing: every place matters to someone. That defunct grain elevator on the edge of town? It might be an urban explorer's paradise. That old bridge? It could be heritage documentation. That construction site showing the evolution of a neighborhood? It's social history in the making.

The corporate approach treats our world like a product catalog. We're here to treat it like what it actually is—a shared human experience worth documenting in all its messy, beautiful, complicated glory.

## The Democratization Advantage: Why Community-Generated Matters

Here's where things get really interesting. When your 3D data comes from the community, you get advantages that corporate mapping can never provide:

**Hyperlocal Expertise**: Your neighbor who's been photographing the same intersection for years might notice details that corporate cameras miss. The way afternoon light hits the old brick wall. How the seasonal flooding affects the underpass. The subtle changes that only someone who actually lives there would care about.

**Cultural Sensitivity**: Communities can document places with the respect and context they deserve, rather than how a distant corporation thinks they should be represented. Sacred spaces, culturally significant areas, places with complicated histories—all documented with the nuance they require.

**Temporal Accuracy**: Corporate maps update on corporate timelines. Your community map updates when people notice changes. That new construction project? Documented in real-time by the people watching it happen. That seasonal event? Captured when it actually matters to the people who attend.

**Democratic Prioritization**: Instead of algorithmic decisions about what deserves high resolution, communities decide collectively what they want to see clearly. Maybe that small town square gets detailed scanning because it's where the weekly farmers market draws the whole community together.
