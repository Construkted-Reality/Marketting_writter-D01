# The People's Globe: Why Your 3D Scans Matter More Than You Think

**By Construkted Reality Team**  
**December 15, 2024**  
**Reading Time: 7 minutes**

Ever wondered what happens when millions of people decide to map the world themselves? We're about to find out.

Picture this: Instead of relying on Google's satellite imagery or Microsoft's street view cameras, what if we had a digital Earth built by the people, for the people? A globe where every hill, every building, every archaeological site was captured by someone who actually cared enough to document it. No corporate algorithms deciding what gets included. No paywalls blocking access to your own neighborhood. No algorithms deciding what "important" looks like.

Welcome to our vision of the Construkted Globe—where the world's 3D data belongs to the people who created it.

## The Corporate Mapping Problem (And Why It Drives Us Crazy)

Here's the thing about current digital mapping: it's beautifully curated, incredibly comprehensive, and completely controlled by a handful of mega-corporations. Google Earth shows us the world, but through Google's lens. Bing Maps gives us navigation, but with Microsoft's priorities. These aren't bad products—they're just not *ours*.

Think about it: When was the last time you saw a hyperlocal archaeological site on Google Earth? Or a detailed 3D scan of that small historical building your grandmother mentioned? Corporate mapping focuses on what's commercially relevant, not what's culturally significant or personally meaningful to communities.

The market research tells a sobering story. While enterprise GIS solutions like ArcGIS cost $25,000 annually for small teams, and even "affordable" alternatives like DroneDeploy run $17,000+ per year, the vast majority of detailed 3D documentation happens in silos. Individual surveyors, archaeologists, and hobbyists capture incredible data that never sees the light of day because sharing it requires expensive software, complex workflows, or corporate approval.

## Where We Are Now: The Early Days of Digital Democracy

Right now, the Construkted Globe is in its infancy—and that's exactly what makes it magical. We're not trying to compete with Google's satellite coverage or Microsoft's street-level imagery. We're building something entirely different: a living, breathing documentation of the world as seen through human eyes.

What do we have so far? A growing collection of 3D scans that tell stories corporate mapping never could:

- **Archaeological sites** captured by field teams who understand their historical significance
- **Construction progress documentation** from contractors who know which details matter
- **Environmental monitoring data** from consultants tracking real changes over time
- **Artistic interpretations** from creators who see beauty where others see data points
- **Community landmarks** that locals treasure but corporate maps ignore

Each contribution is geolocated, timestamped, and rich with metadata that tells not just *what* was scanned, but *why* it matters to the person who scanned it. This isn't just 3D data—it's human data with context, meaning, and community significance.

Here's what excites us most: we have no idea what the Construkted Globe will become. Unlike corporate mapping projects with predetermined priorities and standardized capture protocols, our globe evolves organically based on what our community cares about.

Maybe a group of mining engineers will contribute detailed scans of safety-critical infrastructure, creating the world's first crowdsourced safety documentation system. ## Why This Matters More Than You Think

Let's talk about why a user-generated 3D globe matters in 2024. The traditional mapping industry is worth billions, but it's controlled by a few players making decisions for everyone else. When corporate algorithms decide what gets documented, they prioritize commercial value over community value, population density over cultural significance, and algorithmic efficiency over human meaning.

Our research shows that 85% of geospatial professionals struggle with collaboration challenges. They spend hours compressing shapefiles, emailing large files back and forth, and managing manual version control. Meanwhile, incredible 3D data sits trapped in individual systems, never contributing to a larger picture.

The Construkted Globe solves this by design. When you contribute a scan, it doesn't just sit in your project folder—it becomes part of a living, growing documentation of human space. That archaeological site you scanned doesn't just help your current project; it becomes a permanent record for future researchers, a teaching tool for students, and a celebration of human heritage.

## The Technology That Makes It Possible

Under the hood, we've built something that handles the complexity so you don't have to. Our platform accepts virtually any 3D format—E57, LAS, LAZ, IFC, RVT—and automatically processes it for web viewing. No more software licensing headaches or format conversion nightmares.

The smart file management system handles datasets ranging from small architectural scans to massive LiDAR surveys. Point cloud files routinely reach dozens of gigabytes, but our chunked upload system means you can start a scan upload from your field laptop and finish it on your office desktop without losing progress.

Most importantly, everything works through a standard web browser. No special GIS software required. No technical expertise needed. Just upload, and your contribution becomes part of the global collection.

## What Makes This Different from Corporate Mapping

Corporate mapping focuses on comprehensive coverage. User-generated mapping focuses on meaningful coverage. When a construction company documents their building progress, they're capturing details that matter to future contractors, inspectors, and historians. When an environmental consultant scans a wetland restoration site, they're creating a baseline for ecological monitoring. When an art student documents their neighborhood, they're preserving community memory.

This isn't about replacing satellite imagery or street-level photography. It's about complementing corporate data with human context. The Construkted Globe becomes more valuable not despite its gaps, but because of them. The areas that don't have corporate coverage become opportunities for community documentation. The places corporate algorithms deem "unimportant" become exactly the places where human stories unfold.

## The Network Effect Magic

Here's where it gets really interesting: the more data we have, the more valuable every individual contribution becomes. A single archaeological scan is interesting. A hundred archaeological scans across a region create patterns. A thousand scans across a continent reveal migration patterns, cultural connections, and historical narratives impossible to see in isolation.

The network effect means your contribution isn't just adding to the pile—it's making every other contribution more valuable. That construction site documentation you uploaded? It becomes part of a time-lapse showing how cities evolve. The environmental monitoring data? It contributes to understanding climate change impacts at the local level.

This is why we say the Construkted Globe belongs to its contributors. Unlike corporate mapping where users are consumers, here users are co-creators building something none of us could build alone.

## Your Data, Your Rules

Here's what we promise: your contributions remain yours. We're not selling your data to advertisers or using it to train AI models for corporate profit. The scans you contribute to the Construkted Globe are part of a public good, accessible to researchers, educators, and community members worldwide.

You maintain control over visibility—public contributions appear on the global globe, while private projects stay within your team. But when you choose to share publicly, you're contributing to humanity's collective knowledge about the spaces we inhabit.

The only restriction? The scans must be of real places. No fictional worlds, no artistic interpretations of non-existent spaces. We're building a documentation of actual human geography, one scan at a time.
