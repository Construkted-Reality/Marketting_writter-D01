## The Corporate Cartel Problem

Let's be honest—mapping has become a playground for the big boys. Google, Microsoft, Esri, and a handful of other giants control how we see our world digitally. They decide what gets mapped, how accurate it is, who gets access, and what it costs. Want detailed 3D data of that archaeological site you're working on? Better budget $25,000 a year for ArcGIS. Need to share your drone survey with stakeholders? Hope you enjoy managing software licenses and file version hell.

Here's what the market research tells us: **85% of users complain about collaboration challenges** with existing tools. We're talking about people literally emailing massive shapefiles back and forth, naming versions like "road_network_Jan2018_v1_final_FINAL2.xlsx." Sound familiar?

The traditional players have created a world where:
- **Small teams spend $25,000+ annually** just to access professional mapping tools
- **Data gets trapped** in proprietary formats controlled by single companies
- **Community knowledge** gets locked behind paywalls and corporate agendas
- **Real-world places** get mapped according to what serves business interests, not human curiosity

We needed a different approach. Not just better software, but a fundamentally different relationship between people and their digital representation of Earth.

## Our Radical Experiment: The People's Globe

The Construkted Globe is our answer to the corporate mapping monopoly. Think of it as Wikipedia meets Google Earth, but for 3D data. Every scan, every measurement, every digital twin comes from real people—architects scanning their latest project, surveyors documenting construction progress, hobbyists exploring their hometown, archaeologists preserving ancient sites.

Here's what makes this different:

**No Corporate Overlords**: Unlike Google Earth or Bing Maps, nobody's deciding your data serves their advertising model or business strategy. When you contribute a scan of your local park, it stays there for the community to explore, learn from, and build upon.

**Real Places, Real People**: Every point in our globe represents someone's actual work. That 3D scan of the downtown bridge? Uploaded by a structural engineer. The detailed model of that historic building? Created by a heritage preservationist. The underground mine mapping? Contributed by safety-conscious mining professionals.

**Community-Driven Accuracy**: Remember when Google Earth showed your house with a 50-foot error? In our model, local experts correct and enhance the data. If a surveyor notices an error in their area, they can update it. If an architect adds new building details, everyone benefits.

The implications go way beyond pretty 3D visualizations. We're talking about:

**Democratized Knowledge**: That grad student in Nepal can explore detailed 3D scans of European cathedrals without paying $10,000 for software licenses. Communities can document their heritage before it disappears. Researchers can access real-world data for studies that would otherwise be impossible.

**Collaborative Science**: Environmental consultants monitoring deforestation can instantly share detailed site data with regulatory agencies. Construction teams can compare progress across multiple projects in real-time. Archaeologists can collaborate on site documentation regardless of their institutional affiliations.

**Future-Proofing Heritage**: As climate change and development threaten historical sites, we're creating a permanent digital record accessible to future generations, regardless of what happens to the physical locations.

2. **Smart Placement**: GPS metadata automatically places your scan on the globe. No manual coordinate wrangling required.
