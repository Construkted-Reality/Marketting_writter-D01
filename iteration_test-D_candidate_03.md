## The Corporate Cartography Problem

Let's be honest—current digital mapping has become a corporate oligopoly. Google controls what we see of the world through their satellite imagery and Street View. Microsoft decides how we explore cities through Bing Maps. These platforms, while impressive, have fundamental limitations:

**They're gatekeepers.** Want to contribute your detailed 3D scan of a historic building? Too bad—that's not in their roadmap. Need to share precise survey data for a construction project? Hope you have an enterprise contract.

**They're black boxes.** Ever tried to understand why certain areas are blurry on Google Earth? Or why some neighborhoods get updated imagery while others don't? You're not supposed to question the algorithm.

**They're extractive.** Your local knowledge, your detailed scans, your community's insights—they all become corporate assets, monetized and controlled by people who've never set foot in your neighborhood.

We've seen this movie before. Remember when the internet was supposed to be decentralized and democratizing? Now a few platforms control most of our digital lives. We don't want that future for how we map and understand our physical world.

## Why User-Generated 3D Matters

Here's the thing that corporate mapping gets wrong: **Local expertise is irreplaceable.** 

A professional surveyor knows which elevation changes matter most for drainage on a construction site. An environmental consultant understands which vegetation patterns indicate ecosystem health. A heritage preservationist recognizes which architectural details document historical significance.

These aren't just data points—they're stories. And stories need storytellers.

When we combine millions of these local perspectives, something magical happens. We don't just get a more accurate map—we get a richer, more nuanced understanding of our world. The corporate algorithm might see "residential building," but your neighbor's 3D scan reveals the community garden behind it, the storm drainage patterns that prevent flooding, and the architectural details that make your neighborhood unique.
