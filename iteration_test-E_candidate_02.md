# The People's Globe: Building a User-Generated Digital Earth

*By Construkted Reality Team*  
*March 15, 2024*  
*Reading time: 7 minutes*

## When Maps Belong to Everyone

Picture this: You're exploring a remote mountain village in Peru, zooming in on Google Earth to get a feel for the terrain before your research trip. But what you see is a blurry, outdated satellite image from 2018. The streets are there, but the new community center isn't. The recent landslide that changed the river's path? Nowhere to be found. The beautiful 3D scan of the local cathedral that a digital heritage team just completed? Locked away in their proprietary platform.

This is the reality of our current digital Earth—powerful, yes, but controlled by a handful of corporations making decisions about what gets mapped, how often it gets updated, and who gets access to the detailed, accurate information we need.

At Construkted Reality, we believe there's a better way. We're building something radical: the Construkted Globe—a living, breathing digital Earth created entirely by people like you. No corporate overlords deciding what matters. No paywalls for detailed imagery. No algorithms deciding what gets updated and what gets forgotten.

## The State of Our Globe Today

Right now, the Construkted Globe is like a newborn baby—full of potential but still finding its feet. We've got a growing collection of contributed 3D scans from our community: everything from detailed interior scans of historical buildings to comprehensive drone surveys of construction sites, from archaeological documentation of ancient ruins to artistic explorations of urban spaces.

Each scan comes with rich metadata—the when, where, and how of its creation. A drone survey of a mining site in Australia carries information about the equipment used, the flight parameters, and processing details. A photogrammetric scan of a medieval cathedral in France includes the camera positions, lighting conditions, and measurement references. These aren't just pretty 3D models—they're comprehensive digital records of our physical world.

But here's where it gets exciting: every scan a user contributes doesn't just sit in isolation. It becomes part of a living, collaborative platform where multiple datasets can be layered, compared, and analyzed together. That mining site survey? It might be combined with environmental monitoring data, or compared with previous surveys to track changes over time. That cathedral scan? It could be integrated with historical documentation, architectural plans, or even sound recordings to create a comprehensive digital preservation record.

## Why a User-Generated Globe Matters

You might be wondering: "Why does this matter? Don't we already have Google Earth?" Here's where the rubber meets the road—or rather, where the community meets the corporate map.

**Corporate platforms prioritize what's profitable, not what's important.** A major corporation might focus on mapping high-income neighborhoods and popular tourist destinations, leaving vast swaths of the world under-documented or outdated. Meanwhile, a small community in rural Guatemala might have critical infrastructure projects that desperately need detailed, accurate 3D documentation—but those won't appear on corporate platforms until they become "commercially relevant."

**User-generated mapping is democratized mapping.** When anyone can contribute detailed scans of anywhere, we start to see a more complete picture of our world. The remote research station in Antarctica gets the same attention as a downtown skyscraper. The Indigenous cultural site gets preserved in the same detail as a corporate headquarters.

**Community-driven data stays community-controlled.** When you contribute to the Construkted Globe, you're not giving away your data to a corporation that might monetize it, restrict access to it, or even delete it when it's no longer "strategically valuable." Your contributions remain part of a collective resource that serves the community that created it.

**Innovation happens at the edges, not the center.** Many of the most exciting developments in geospatial technology come from researchers, small companies, and independent creators who don't fit neatly into corporate roadmaps. A user-generated globe creates space for these edge innovations to flourish and connect with the mainstream.

## The Future We're Building Together

Imagine a globe where:
- A heritage organization in Kenya can document and preserve archaeological sites with the same detail and accessibility as a European museum
- Environmental researchers can layer decades of ecosystem monitoring data with current high-resolution scans to track changes and predict future impacts
- Urban planners can visualize proposed developments in the context of existing 3D documentation, making better-informed decisions
- Indigenous communities can maintain digital sovereignty over their cultural sites and territories
- Artists and creators can build immersive experiences using real-world data, fostering new forms of digital expression

This isn't just a map—it's a platform for human knowledge, creativity, and collaboration. As more people contribute, the globe becomes richer, more detailed, and more useful for everyone. It's a classic network effect, but instead of serving corporate interests, it serves human curiosity and the collective need to understand and preserve our world.

## The Power of "No Restrictions" (Within Reason)

We mean it when we say "no restrictions"—with one important exception: the scans have to be of real places. Why such a seemingly open policy? Because we believe that artificial barriers often prevent the most interesting and valuable contributions.

Maybe you're a hobbyist who scanned your neighborhood playground and want to share it with other parents. Perhaps you're a student who documented a local historical building for a class project. Or maybe you're part of an artist collective creating site-specific installations and want to share your process with the broader creative community.

All of these contributions matter. The playground scan might help other parents assess accessibility features. The historical building documentation might assist preservation efforts. The artistic documentation might inspire other creators or provide context for understanding the piece.

By removing barriers to contribution, we're not just building a database—we're fostering a culture of sharing, collaboration, and mutual support. We're saying that everyone's perspective on the world is valuable, and everyone deserves to see their corner of the globe represented with the same care and detail as any other.

## How You Can Help Build the Globe

The Construkted Globe is only as good as the contributions we receive. Every scan, every dataset, every piece of documentation adds a new thread to our collective tapestry of human knowledge.

**Got 3D data collecting dust on a hard drive?** Upload it. That laser scan of your company's facility that you're not sure what to do with? The photogrammetric model you created for a class project? The drone survey you did for a friend who's building a house? Share it with the world.

**Working on a project that involves 3D documentation?** Make the Construkted Globe part of your workflow from the start. Build collaboration into your process. Let your clients know that their project will become part of a larger community resource.

**Teaching or training others in 3D scanning or GIS?** Encourage your students to contribute their projects to the globe. Help them see their work as part of a larger conversation about how we document and understand our world.

**Just curious about what's possible?** Explore the globe. See what others have contributed. Get inspired by the diversity of projects and approaches. Then consider how you might contribute your own unique perspective.

## A Globe by the People, for the People

In a world increasingly dominated by corporate-controlled digital spaces, the Construkted Globe represents something different: a truly community-driven resource that serves human needs rather than quarterly reports. It's a digital commons where knowledge flows freely, where collaboration happens organically, and where the map itself becomes a reflection of our collective humanity.

We're not saying corporate mapping platforms don't have their place—they do important work and serve important functions. But we believe there's room for something more: a globe that belongs to all of us, where every contribution matters, and where the only agenda is expanding our collective understanding of the world we share.

The future of digital mapping isn't just about better technology or more detailed imagery—it's about who gets to participate in creating our digital Earth. With the Construkted Globe, we're inviting everyone to be a cartographer, everyone to be a documentarian, everyone to be a guardian of our shared spatial heritage.

Your corner of the world deserves to be seen. The Construkted Globe is waiting for your contribution.
