# Building Tomorrow's Digital Earth: The Story of the Construkted Globe

*By Construkted Reality Team | December 19, 2024 | 12 min read*

Ever stared at Google Earth and wondered, "What if this belonged to all of us instead of just shareholders?" 

We have. That's exactly why we're building the Construkted Globe—and why we need your help to make it real.

Picture this: A digital Earth where every hill, building, street, and hidden corner has been mapped by real people with real passion for the places they love. Not by corporate committees deciding what deserves to be seen, but by the surveyor documenting a mining site, the architect preserving a historic building, the drone pilot mapping their hometown, the archaeology student documenting an ancient site.

Welcome to our vision of a truly democratic digital globe.

## The Problem with Corporate Cartography

Let's be honest—current mapping platforms are amazing, but they're not *ours*. When you use Google Earth or Bing Maps, you're looking through a lens that big corporations have carefully curated, updated, and controlled according to their business models and priorities.

Want to see that construction site your friend is working on? Too bad—satellite imagery might be years old, and street-level data might not exist at all. Interested in that amazing old building that's about to be demolished? Corporate mapping services decide if it's "worth" capturing in high detail.

**Here's what really gets us**: The technology to create detailed, accurate 3D maps has existed for years. Drones, photogrammetry, LiDAR scanning—these tools are in the hands of millions of people worldwide. But where does that data go? Into proprietary silos, locked behind expensive licenses, scattered across hard drives, or worse, lost forever when projects end.

The result? We're living in a world with unprecedented mapping capabilities, but our digital Earth is still largely corporate-controlled and incomplete.

## Enter the Construkted Globe: Our Digital Democracy

The Construkted Globe is our answer to this problem. Think of it as the world's first truly user-generated digital Earth—where every 3D scan, every point cloud, every detailed model contributed by our community becomes part of a living, breathing global map.

### What Makes This Different?

**Corporate mapping platforms** show you what they think you should see. **The Construkted Globe** shows you what real people have documented because it mattered to them.

**Corporate mapping platforms** update on their schedule. **The Construkted Globe** updates continuously as our community contributes new data.

**Corporate mapping platforms** decide what gets high detail. **The Construkted Globe** gives every place the attention it deserves, from major cities to forgotten rural cemeteries.

Here's the beautiful part: We don't restrict what gets uploaded (as long as it's real places). That construction site your friend mentioned? When someone scans it and uploads to Construkted Reality, it becomes part of our global record. That historic building about to be demolished? When an architecture student documents it in 3D, it's preserved forever in our digital Earth.

## Where We Are Today: Building the Foundation

Right now, the Construkted Globe is in its early stages, and honestly? It's absolutely thrilling to watch it grow.

We've got community members from every corner contributing: surveyors uploading mining site scans, drone pilots sharing neighborhood mapping projects, architects contributing building documentation, archaeology students preserving excavation sites, and hobbyists documenting everything from local parks to abandoned structures.

**The magic is already happening**: When a user uploads a 3D scan of their local downtown, other community members can explore it, add annotations, measure distances, or use it as reference for their own projects. When someone documents a historic building, it becomes a resource for preservationists worldwide. When a construction team uploads progress scans, it creates a permanent record of how our built environment evolves.

But here's the thing—this is just the beginning. With every upload, every contribution, we're not just adding data points to a map. We're building something unprecedented: a digital Earth that belongs to its contributors.

## The Anti-Corporate Advantage: Why Open Matters

Here's something that might surprise you: **We don't want to control what gets mapped**.

Corporate mapping services have to make decisions about where to allocate resources. They prioritize profitable markets, high-traffic areas, or locations that serve their broader business goals. Remote rural areas? Probably not high on their list. Small towns? Might get basic coverage at best. That interesting geological formation near your hiking trail? Unlikely to get detailed attention.

But when mapping becomes community-driven? Suddenly every place has advocates. The local historical society can document their town square. The environmental club can map the creek behind the school. The drone enthusiast can preserve the abandoned railroad bridge before it collapses.

**This is what we mean by "no restrictions other than real places"**: We want to see your town, your neighborhood, your favorite hiking trail, that interesting building you pass every day, the construction site across from your office, the archaeological site you're studying, the mining operation you're surveying.

Every place matters to someone. And in our digital Earth, every place can be documented.
